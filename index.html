The RH Smith Chart

The classical Smith chart is the Möbius map Γ=(Z−1)/(Z+1): it folds the right half-plane of positive-real impedances into the unit disk, so passivity becomes something you can see. The Riemann zeta function admits the same move. Li's map z = 1 − 1/s folds the critical strip around the unit circle: RH is exactly the statement that every nontrivial zero sits on the boundary — and the two positivity diagnostics below (Li coefficients λn, Toeplitz truncations A(m) of the Weil form) are the circle-side instruments that would catch one leaving it.

INSTRUMENT, NOT EVIDENCE — this page proves nothing about RH and displays no evidence for or against it. All 100,000 zeros used here are known to lie on the critical line (Odlyzko). The red "planted violation" is a synthetic, counterfactual zero inserted by hand to show what a violation would look like on each gauge. Both encodings are RH-equivalent re-coordinatizations [T: Li 1997 / Bombieri–Lagarias; Paper 14], not new mathematics.

Third meter — Suzuki's Θω inner function (the all-pass gauge)

Θω(z) = ξ(½−ω−iz)/ξ(½+ω−iz), rendered here as a truncated Blaschke product over the 100k charted zeros [instrument approximation, labeled]. Unconditionally |Θω| = 1 on the real axis — in network terms it is an all-pass filter. Suzuki (arXiv:1204.1827) ties RH to Θω being a meromorphic inner function: |Θω| ≤ 1 on the whole upper half-plane. An off-line zero with β > ω puts a pole in the upper half-plane and the gauge pegs. Trace shown along the probe line Im z = 0.05 at ω = 0.05; the planted β=0.1 violation peaks at |Θ| ≈ 41.8 over the pole at x ≈ −98.83. Instrument lesson [measured]: unlike λn and μmin, this gauge is local — a coarse probe grid (spacing 0.25) missed the pole entirely; the violation's width is ~(β−ω). Sensitivity floor: β ≤ ω violations keep all poles below the axis and do not peg this meter (verified by control test) — sweeping ω downward is the detection strategy.
